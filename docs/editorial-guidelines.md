## The PHP Foundation — Guest Blog Editorial Guidelines

Thank you for your interest in contributing to The PHP Foundation blog. Our blog is one of the primary ways we communicate with the PHP community, share technical knowledge, and demonstrate the ongoing impact of the Foundation's work. We welcome thoughtful contributions from community members, sponsors, and collaborators.

These guidelines exist to make the process smooth for everyone and to ensure that everything published on our blog reflects the quality and values our readers expect.


### Who We Write For

Our readers include PHP developers of all experience levels, open source contributors, framework maintainers, sponsors and organizations that rely on PHP, and people generally interested in the health of the PHP ecosystem. Many are highly technical; some are not. A good post speaks clearly to both.


### What We Publish

We welcome posts in the following categories:

* **Technical deep-dives**: Detailed explorations of a PHP feature, PHP extension, RFC, or related topic. These are typically written by someone with direct involvement in the subject: a core contributor, RFC author, or experienced practitioner.

* **Ecosystem stories**: How your organization uses PHP, how you migrated to a new version, how you integrated a tool like PIE or Composer, lessons learned from a real-world project.

* **Community and Foundation news**: Announcements, event recaps, sponsor perspectives, and updates relevant to the PHP community.

* **Opinion and analysis**: Thoughtful takes on the direction of PHP, open source sustainability, language design, or related topics. These carry the author's byline and perspective, not the Foundation's position.

We do **not** publish:

* Promotional content for commercial products or services, or requests for employment
* Content that has been published elsewhere (we require first publication rights)
* Posts that are primarily AI-generated without meaningful human authorship and editorial review
* Content unrelated to PHP, open source, or the web development ecosystem

If you're unsure whether your idea fits, just ask. We'd rather have the conversation than have you spend time on something we can't use.


### Length

Length should match the complexity of the topic, not a word count target. That said, here are useful benchmarks:

* **Announcements and updates:** 300–600 words
* **Community stories and opinion pieces:** 600–1,000 words
* **Technical tutorials and deep-dives:** 1,000–2,000 words

Posts longer than 2,000 words are occasionally appropriate for very complex topics, but ask yourself whether the content could be broken into a series instead. Shorter, focused posts often perform better with readers than exhaustive ones.

If code snippets are included in your post, they do not count toward overall number of words in your article.


### Tone and Voice

* Write in **first person** where possible. "I built this" or "we discovered" is more engaging than "the developer built this."
* Be **conversational but authoritative.** You're writing for peers, not a textbook or a press release.
* **Technical accuracy comes first.** If you're explaining how something works, get it right. We'd rather push back on a draft than publish something that is incorrect.
* **Avoid marketing language.** Phrases like "game-changing," "revolutionary," or "best-in-class" don't belong here. Let the work speak for itself.
* **Be direct.** Don't bury the point. State what the post is about in the opening paragraph.


### Accuracy and Attribution

* All factual claims should be verifiable. Link to source material, RFCs, GitHub issues, or documentation where relevant.
* Code examples should be tested and correct.
* If you're referencing someone else's work, attribute it properly. Link to the original source.
* If your post discusses a PHP RFC or feature, link to the relevant wiki page or pull request.
* Do not reproduce substantial portions of copyrighted material.


### Conflict of Interest and Transparency

We believe in transparency. If you work for a company that sponsors the PHP Foundation, or if the post relates to a project your employer benefits from commercially, please disclose this in your author bio or in the post itself. This doesn't disqualify you; it's just honest.

Guest posts represent the author's views, not the official position of The PHP Foundation, unless explicitly stated otherwise.


### The Publishing Process

Here's how it typically works:



1. **The idea surfaces**, usually through a conversation with someone at the Foundation. If you have an idea you'd like to propose, reach out to us at contact@thephp.foundation. 

2. **We discuss the idea**, We'll talk through the angle, the audience, and what would make it a strong post. We may suggest a different framing or scope. 

3. **You share a draft in a Google Doc.** This makes collaboration easy and keeps everything in one place. If Google Docs are not available or accessible, other collaborative platforms can be considered. 

4. **We collaborate on the draft**. A Foundation team member will review the draft and leave comments. This is a collaborative process, not a gatekeeping one. We're trying to make your post the best it can be, not rewrite it in our voice. 

5. **You approve the final version and submit a PR to The PHP Foundation repo**. This should be a Markdown file and should adhere to our guidelines for posting a blog post. You should also include drafts of suggested social media announcements in your PR. (These can be in the comments section.)

6. **The Executive Director publishes the post.** Once it's ready, we'll publish it and share it on our social channels. We'll let you know as soon as it's live. 

**Typical turnaround:** From draft to publication is usually 2–4 weeks, depending on revision cycles and our publishing schedule. If your post is time-sensitive (tied to a release, event, or announcement), let us know early so we can plan accordingly.


### Author Bio

Each post includes a short author bio (2–4 sentences). Please include:

* Your name and how you'd like to be identified
* Your role or affiliation (company, project, or "independent contributor")
* A link to your GitHub, LinkedIn profile, personal site, or other social profile if you'd like one included
* (Optional) A headshot


### After Publication

* The post lives permanently on the PHP Foundation blog at [thephp.foundation/blog](https://thephp.foundation/blog).
* We'll share it on our social channels. We'd love it if you share it too.
* Comments and corrections: if you spot an error after publication, let us know via a PR to the original post.
